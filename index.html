English Phonetics

English has around 44 distinct sounds – far more than its 26 letters suggest. This guide walks you through every vowel and consonant of the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) as used in standard British and American English, with clear notes on how each sound is made and how it is typically spelled.

Monophthongs — Pure Vowel Sounds

A monophthong (from Greek monos "single" + phthongos "sound") is a vowel whose quality remains constant throughout its duration. Your tongue and lips hold a steady position from beginning to end, unlike a diphthong where they glide. English has 12 monophthongs in standard accents, arranged here from the front of the mouth (lips spread) to the back (lips rounded).

/iː/
see

The highest and most front vowel in English. To produce it, spread your lips into a gentle smile and raise the front of your tongue as high as possible without touching the roof of your mouth. Hold the position steady — the ː symbol means it is a long vowel.

Common spellings:

  • ee — see, feet, queen
  • ea — tea, beach, read
  • ie / ei — field, receive
  • e-e — theme, these
/ɪ/
sit

A short, lax vowel produced with the tongue slightly lower and less far forward than /iː/. The lips are neutral — neither spread nor rounded. It is one of the most frequent vowels in English and appears in many unstressed syllables.

Common spellings:

  • i in a closed syllable — sit, bin, fish
  • y — gym, myth, crystal
  • ui — build, guilt
/e/
bed

A mid-front, unrounded vowel. The tongue is at mid-height and pushed forward; lips are spread but relaxed. It is short and appears most often before consonants. In some older transcription systems it is written /ɛ/.

Common spellings:

  • e in a closed syllable — bed, men, help
  • ea — head, bread, meant
  • ai / ay — said, says
/æ/
cat

A low front vowel — the front of the tongue dips toward the floor of the mouth while the lips spread wide. It sounds "sharper" or "brighter" than the similar /ɑː/. In American English it is often lengthened before nasal consonants (man, can't).

Common spellings:

  • a before many consonants — cat, hand, back
  • au — rare: laugh
/ɑː/
car

A long, open back vowel. The mouth is wide open, the jaw drops, and the tongue lies low and back. In non-rhotic accents (e.g. most British English) the /r/ after it is silent, leaving a pure long vowel. In American English the /r/ is usually audible.

Common spellings:

  • ar — car, start, dark
  • a before /f s θ/ in British English — bath, pass, path
  • al — calm, palm, half
/ɒ/
lot

A short, open-back rounded vowel — predominantly a British English sound. The tongue is back and low, and the lips are slightly rounded. In General American English this vowel has merged with /ɑː/, so lot and car use the same vowel quality.

Common spellings:

  • o in a closed syllable — lot, hot, dog
  • a — want, was, watch
  • ou — cough, trough
/ɔː/
law

A long back vowel with noticeable lip rounding. The back of the tongue is raised mid-high. In rhotic accents (American English) this vowel often becomes /ɔːr/ before /r/ as in more, and in many dialects it has merged with /ɑː/ (the "cot-caught merger").

Common spellings:

  • aw / aw-e — law, saw, awe
  • au / augh — cause, caught, daughter
  • or — more, shore, born
  • al — tall, call, walk
/ʊ/
book

A short, lax, back-rounded vowel. The tongue is high and back but not as extreme as for /uː/; the lips are loosely rounded. Often called the "lax u." Learners sometimes confuse it with /uː/ — compare full vs. fool.

Common spellings:

  • oo — book, foot, cook
  • u — put, push, full, bull
  • ou — could, would, should
/uː/
moon

The highest, most back rounded vowel in English. Lips are firmly rounded and pushed forward; the back of the tongue is raised toward the velum (soft palate). It is long — the twin of /iː/ at the front. In some modern accents it is becoming more central (fronting).

Common spellings:

  • oo / oo-e — moon, food, move, hooves
  • ew / ue — new, blue, true
  • o-e / ou — prove, soup, through
  • u-e — rule, rude, June
/ʌ/
cup

A short central-to-back vowel with no lip rounding. The tongue is in a mid-low position. It is the "default" stressed vowel in many English words and is sometimes called the "stressed schwa." Found only in stressed syllables; compare the unstressed /ə/ below.

Common spellings:

  • u — cup, run, fun
  • o — love, come, done, son
  • ou — young, touch, couple
/ɜː/
bird

A long central vowel — the tongue sits roughly in the middle of the mouth, neither front nor back, and the lips are unrounded. In non-rhotic accents the following /r/ is silent and only the long vowel remains. It is the sound heard in her, sir, fur in British English.

Common spellings:

  • ir — bird, first, girl
  • er — her, fern, serve
  • ur — fur, burn, turn
  • or / ear — word, learn
/ə/
about

The schwa — the most common vowel sound in English. It is a mid-central, completely relaxed vowel produced with the tongue at rest and the jaw barely open. It occurs only in unstressed syllables. Almost any vowel letter can reduce to schwa in connected speech. Recognising it is key to natural rhythm.

Common spellings (all unstressed):

  • a — about, sofa
  • e — open, listen
  • o — compose, method
  • u — support, circus

Diphthongs — Gliding Vowel Sounds

A diphthong (from Greek di "two" + phthongos "sound") begins at one vowel position and glides smoothly into another within the same syllable. Think of the movement in boy: your tongue starts open and back, then glides up and forward. English has 8 diphthongs in standard accents — five "closing" diphthongs (gliding toward /ɪ/ or /ʊ/) and three "centring" diphthongs (gliding toward the schwa /ə/).

Closing Diphthongs — glide toward /ɪ/ or /ʊ/
/eɪ/
day

Starts at a mid-front position (like /e/ in bed) and glides upward toward /ɪ/. It is the usual pronunciation of the letter a in an open syllable. Sometimes called the "long a" sound. The first element is strongly stressed; the /ɪ/ glide is brief.

Common spellings:

  • a-e — make, gate, came
  • ay — day, play, say
  • ai — rain, wait, main
  • eigh — eight, weight, neigh
/aɪ/
time

Begins low and open (near /a/) and glides upward and forward toward /ɪ/. The jaw drops wide at the start, then rises as the tongue moves forward. Often called the "long i" sound. It is also the name of the letter I itself.

Common spellings:

  • i-e — time, ride, five
  • igh — night, right, high
  • y / ie — fly, by, pie, tie
  • i (open syllable) — final, pilot
/ɔɪ/
boy

Starts from a back rounded position (like /ɔː/ in law) and glides to /ɪ/. Lips begin rounded then spread as the tongue moves forward. This diphthong has notably consistent spelling — almost always oi or oy — making it one of the easier ones for learners.

Common spellings:

  • oi — oil, coin, voice
  • oy — boy, toy, enjoy
/aʊ/
now

Begins low and open (near /a/) and glides backward and upward toward /ʊ/ — the tongue moves to the back of the mouth and the lips gradually round. The jaw starts wide open and closes as the diphthong progresses. Also called the "ow" sound.

Common spellings:

  • ou — house, out, cloud
  • ow — now, cow, town
/əʊ/
go

Begins at a mid-central position (the schwa /ə/) and glides backward and upward toward /ʊ/. In British English this is the "long o" sound. In American English the starting position is often slightly higher — transcribed /oʊ/ — giving a quality closer to pure back-rounded /o/.

Common spellings:

  • o-e — home, stone, bone
  • oa — road, boat, coat
  • ow — show, know, blow
  • o (open syllable) — go, no, so
Centring Diphthongs — glide toward /ə/ (mainly British English)
/ɪə/
here

Starts at the high front position /ɪ/ and glides to the central /ə/. In rhotic accents (American English) this is simply /ɪr/ — an /ɪ/ followed by a rhotic (r-coloured) vowel. In non-rhotic accents the /r/ letter is silent and the centring diphthong /ɪə/ remains.

Common spellings:

  • eer / ere — beer, here, deer
  • ear — ear, fear, hear
  • ier / ia — pier, idea
/eə/
hair

Starts at mid-front /e/ and glides to the central /ə/. In rhotic accents it becomes /er/. It is the vowel in square and bear — words where British English traditionally uses a diphthong but American English often uses the monophthong /er/.

Common spellings:

  • air / are — hair, fair, care, bare
  • ear / ere — bear, wear, there
  • eir — their
/ʊə/
cure

Starts at the high back rounded position /ʊ/ and glides to the central /ə/. In contemporary British English many speakers are replacing this diphthong with the monophthong /ɔː/ — so cure sounds like caw. It is the rarest of the centring diphthongs and becoming less common.

Common spellings:

  • ure — cure, pure, sure
  • oor / our — poor, tour, moor
  • ua — plural

Consonants — 24 Sounds

A consonant is produced by partly or fully obstructing the airflow. Each consonant is described by three features: voicing (do the vocal cords vibrate?), place (where in the mouth is the obstruction?), and manner (how is the airflow restricted — stopped, forced through a narrow gap, etc.). English consonants are arranged below in pairs where possible, with the voiceless sound listed before its voiced partner.

Plosives (Stops) — airflow is fully blocked, then released

In plosives, the airstream is completely stopped at some point in the vocal tract, then suddenly released. English plosives come in voiceless / voiced pairs.

/p/
pen

Voiceless bilabial plosive. Both lips press together to stop air completely. The throat buzzes for the voiced /b/ but stays silent for /p/. At the start of a stressed syllable in English, /p/ is aspirated — a small puff of air follows the release (hold your hand in front of your mouth!).

Common spellings:

  • p — pen, cap, happy
  • pp — supper, stopped
/b/
bed

Voiced bilabial plosive. Same lip closure as /p/, but the vocal cords vibrate throughout. At the end of a word, /b/ may have its voicing reduced (making it sound similar to /p/) — this is called final obstruent devoicing in many languages but is only partial in English.

Common spellings:

  • b — bed, lab, about
  • bb — rubber, rabbit
  • Note: silent before t — doubt, debt, subtle
/t/
top

Voiceless alveolar plosive. The tongue tip touches the alveolar ridge (the bony ledge just behind the upper teeth), stopping air briefly before a sudden release. In American English, a /t/ between vowels is often "flapped" — pronounced more like a quick /d/ (butter, letter, water).

Common spellings:

  • t / tt — top, button, kitten
  • ed (past tense after voiceless sounds) — walked, stopped
  • Silent in — castle, listen, fasten
/d/
dog

Voiced alveolar plosive. Identical placement to /t/ — tongue tip on the alveolar ridge — but with vocal cord vibration. In informal American English, /d/ between vowels can also be flapped, making ladder and latter sound nearly identical.

Common spellings:

  • d / dd — dog, saddle, add
  • ed (past tense after voiced sounds) — called, played
/k/
cat

Voiceless velar plosive. The back of the tongue presses against the velum (soft palate) at the rear of the mouth. Like /p/ and /t/, it is aspirated at the beginning of stressed syllables. Notice how the contact point shifts slightly depending on the following vowel (key vs. cool).

Common spellings:

  • c (before a, o, u, r, l) — cat, cold, cup
  • k — king, make, week
  • ck — back, clock, duck
  • ch — chaos, school, echo
  • qu — quite, queen
/ɡ/
get

Voiced velar plosive. Identical back-of-tongue placement to /k/, but with vibrating vocal cords. A common learner difficulty is the difference between the hard g (/ɡ/ in get) and soft g (/dʒ/ in gem), which depends largely on the following vowel letter.

Common spellings:

  • g (before a, o, u) — get, go, gun
  • gg — egg, bigger
  • gh — ghost, ghoul

Fricatives — air forced through a narrow gap, creating friction

In fricatives, air is forced through a constricted passage, creating a hissing or buzzing sound. English has 9 fricatives — the largest category.

/f/
fish

Voiceless labiodental fricative. The upper front teeth touch or nearly touch the inner lower lip, and air is squeezed between them. This creates a steady, hushing friction — without any vocal cord buzz.

Common spellings:

  • f / ff — fish, off, coffee
  • ph — phone, photo, graph
  • gh (after round vowels) — enough, rough, laugh
/v/
van

Voiced labiodental fricative. Exactly like /f/ in placement, but with the vocal cords buzzing. Place your fingers on your throat — you should feel vibration for /v/ but not /f/. The pair /f/–/v/ is one of the most useful voicing contrasts in English (fine/vine, safe/save).

Common spellings:

  • v — van, love, over
  • f — of (the only common exception)
/θ/
think

Voiceless dental fricative. The tongue tip is placed between or just behind the upper and lower front teeth, and air streams through to create a soft friction. This sound is rare across the world's languages, and many learners substitute /s/, /t/, or /f/. The key: keep the tongue visible between the teeth briefly.

Common spellings:

  • th — think, thank, health, month, teeth
/ð/
this

Voiced dental fricative. Identical tongue position to /θ/ but with vocal cord vibration added. In English, the voiced /ð/ appears mostly in grammatical (function) words — the, this, that, they, them, there, though. Content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives) tend to use voiceless /θ/ instead.

Common spellings:

  • th — this, the, mother, breathe, bathe
/s/
sun

Voiceless alveolar fricative. The tongue tip approaches (but does not touch) the alveolar ridge, and air streams in a sharp hiss through the narrow channel. It is one of the most frequent consonants in English and is also used for plural endings and third-person singular verb forms.

Common spellings:

  • s / ss — sun, glass, miss
  • c (before e, i, y) — circle, city, cycle
  • sc — science, scene
/z/
zoo

Voiced alveolar fricative. The voiced counterpart of /s/. In spoken English, plural and possessive endings spelled -s are often pronounced /z/ after voiced sounds (dogs, beds, cars). The buzzing quality is the distinguishing cue — compare sip vs. zip.

Common spellings:

  • z / zz — zoo, buzz, jazz
  • s (between vowels or after voiced consonant) — nose, is, dogs
  • x — xylophone, example
/ʃ/
ship

Voiceless palato-alveolar fricative. The tongue blade (just behind the tip) approaches the area between the alveolar ridge and the hard palate. The lips protrude slightly forward. The result is a hushing, "shushing" sound — softer and lower-pitched than /s/. Think of telling someone to be quiet: "Shhh…"

Common spellings:

  • sh — ship, wash, fresh
  • ti — nation, station, patient
  • ssi / si — mission, tension
  • ch — machine, chef
/ʒ/
measure

Voiced palato-alveolar fricative. The voiced partner of /ʃ/ — same tongue position, same lip protrusion, but with vocal cord vibration. It is the rarest consonant phoneme in English, appearing mostly in French-origin words. Think of the "zh" sound in the middle of treasure, leisure, vision.

Common spellings:

  • s / si / su — measure, vision, usual
  • g (in French loanwords) — genre, beige, rouge
/h/
hat

Voiceless glottal fricative. Unlike all other fricatives, /h/ has no fixed place of articulation in the mouth — the friction is created at the glottis (the space between the vocal cords) as air rushes through them. The surrounding vowel shapes the mouth position. It only appears before vowels; the letter h is silent in many common words.

Common spellings:

  • h — hat, help, behind
  • Silent h — hour, honest, heir, vehicle

Affricates — a plosive followed immediately by a fricative

Affricates begin with a complete stop (plosive) and release into friction rather than open air — giving a two-part sound treated as a single phoneme.

/tʃ/
chair

Voiceless palato-alveolar affricate. The tongue tip and blade form a complete seal against the alveolar ridge, then release slowly through the /ʃ/ friction position — never letting air through cleanly. Think of /t/ melting into /ʃ/ as a single motion. A very satisfying sound when produced clearly.

Common spellings:

  • ch — chair, church, teacher
  • tch — catch, watch, fetch
  • t before ure/ion — nature, question
/dʒ/
jump

Voiced palato-alveolar affricate. The voiced partner of /tʃ/ — /d/ releases into /ʒ/ with vocal cord vibration throughout. This is the sound of the letter j in English, and also the "soft g" before front vowels. Compare chunk /tʃ/ vs. junk /dʒ/.

Common spellings:

  • j — jump, job, enjoy
  • g (before e, i, y) — gem, giant, gym
  • ge / dge — age, judge, bridge

Nasals — air passes through the nose

In nasals, the mouth is fully blocked (like a plosive) but the soft palate drops, letting all air escape through the nasal passage instead. All English nasals are voiced.

/m/
man

Voiced bilabial nasal. Both lips press together (like /p/ and /b/), but instead of blocking all airflow, the velum lowers to route air through the nose. Hum with your lips closed — that is /m/. It can form a syllable on its own in English (rhythm, prism).

Common spellings:

  • m / mm — man, hammer, lamb
  • Silent b after m — lamb, bomb, thumb
/n/
no

Voiced alveolar nasal. Tongue tip seals against the alveolar ridge (like /t/ and /d/), and air flows through the nose. It is one of the most common consonants in English. Before /k/ or /g/ the nasal often assimilates to /ŋ/ (income, congress).

Common spellings:

  • n / nn — no, sun, funny
  • kn (silent k) — know, knee, knife
  • gn — sign, gnaw
/ŋ/
sing

Voiced velar nasal. The back of the tongue presses against the velum (like /k/ and /ɡ/), while air escapes through the nose. It never appears at the beginning of a word in English. A common variation: in -ing suffixes in casual speech, the /ŋ/ may be replaced by /n/ — the well-known "g-dropping" (e.g. runnin').

Common spellings:

  • ng — sing, long, thing
  • n before /k/ — bank, think, uncle
  • nk — drink, pink, tank

Approximants — articulators approach but don't create friction

Approximants are in between vowels and consonants — the passage narrows but not enough to create turbulence. They are always voiced and often called "semi-vowels" or "glides" (for /w/ and /j/).

/l/
leg

Voiced alveolar lateral approximant. The tongue tip touches the alveolar ridge, but the sides of the tongue stay down — air flows around the sides ("lateral" flow). English has two varieties: "clear l" before vowels (let) and "dark l" (with the back of the tongue raised) before consonants and at word-ends (milk, call).

Common spellings:

  • l / ll — leg, ball, yellow
  • Silent l — calm, talk, half, folk
/r/
red

Voiced alveolar/post-alveolar approximant. The tongue tip curls slightly upward and back (retroflex) without touching anything. The lips may round slightly. In rhotic accents (most of American English) /r/ is pronounced wherever the letter r appears; in non-rhotic accents (most British English) it is only pronounced before vowels.

Common spellings:

  • r / rr / wr — red, arrow, write
  • Silent in non-rhotic accents — car, bird, here
/j/
yet

Voiced palatal approximant. The front of the tongue rises toward the hard palate — the same position as /iː/ — and the mouth glides straight into the following vowel. This is why yet begins with a glide from /iː/-position. Often called the "y-glide." It is the same as the German letter j and Spanish y.

Common spellings:

  • y — yet, year, beyond
  • u / ew / eu — use, few, feud (the /j/ is part of the vowel complex)
  • i — onion, million
/w/
wet

Voiced labio-velar approximant. Two things happen simultaneously: the back of the tongue rises toward the velum (like /uː/) AND the lips round and protrude. Say /uː/ and then immediately open into the following vowel — that movement is /w/. Sometimes called the "w-glide."

Common spellings:

  • w — wet, away, swim
  • wh — when, where, what (many speakers use /w/ here)
  • Silent w — write, two, sword, who
